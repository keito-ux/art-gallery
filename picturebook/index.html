About the Story

One quiet night, a boy meets a glowing star fairy in his room. Entranced, he follows her into a world of wonder. Guided by a gentle reindeer, he journeys through enchanted forests and silent skies, planting a seed that grows into a blooming garden — a symbol of hope and light.

Chasing the star is the journey of following your true calling — the quiet pull of your destiny.
Growing a garden teaches us this: you don’t catch the butterfly by chasing it, but when you gently tend your space, it finds you.

Behind the Scenes

This international picture book project began with two people — Keito from Japan and Yulianna from Ukraine — who first met in Kyiv and later started co-creating online. What started as a simple exchange of art and stories became a heartfelt mission — to create art for support through a picture book, inspired by the spirit of Ukraine, and to open a space for shared expression beyond borders.

Keito Nakanishi

A senior student at Sophia University in Tokyo, majoring in science and engineering. In August 2024, I backpacked through Eastern Europe and was deeply moved by the spirit of the Ukrainian people. That winter, I traveled to Ukraine, visiting Kyiv and Lviv. Although self-taught in oil painting, I create works that reflect quiet beauty and cross-cultural connection — shaped by my travels, encounters, and a deep desire to express warmth through art.

Yulianna

A fourth-year student of Folklore Studies at Taras Shevchenko National University of Kyiv. I've admired art since childhood. I first completed music school (piano), later began attending an art studio. I absolutely love watercolor, though I also enjoy exploring different materials in my artwork. I find great joy in visiting exhibitions and listening to other artists talk about what they create and why. It deeply inspires me. Nature especially moves me – it often becomes the main subject of my drawings and photographs. I sincerely love Ukraine. Studying at university allows me to explore Ukrainian culture on a deeper level. I’m also interested in Asian culture.
Through my artwork, I try to share kindness, warmth and my personal view of the world. I want to create something light. I believe this is one of the goals of the project – to share light, even when surrounded by darkness.

Valentyna

My name is Valentyna, I’m Ukrainian, and I’m 19 years old. I’m an artist by education, holding a diploma of a junior bachelor. This year I graduated from college with a degree in "Fine Arts, Decorative Arts, and Restoration," specializing in painting.

However, I still need to grow and improve, as there is so much I have yet to explore. That’s why I can’t call myself a professional yet — but I’m on my way there.

What inspires me most is seeing beauty in everything — without exaggeration. What helps me create is the understanding that everything around us is part of a system. This awareness brings a deeper sense of value to all living and non-living things.

In my art, I enjoy working with watercolor, graphic materials, and oil painting. I’m most interested in portraying people — their inner world and external uniqueness. I’m inspired by Ukrainian culture, by nature (a truly great teacher), and by my own inner experiences.

I create art because, for me, it’s the best way to show and explain to others how I see and understand certain things in this world.

🇺🇦 Odukalets Yuliya

"The Big Little Things"
In childhood, there was always something that was “off-limits.” Don’t touch that, don’t go there, don’t take this, don’t say that. The adult world seemed full of restrictions, as if we lived among invisible boundaries. And yet, that was exactly when true freedom was born.
Because behind every “don’t” was our quiet “but I’ll try.” We climbed trees, touched forbidden things, sought adventures in the dark corners of the yard. And we discovered — nothing terrible happened. We weren’t punished, the world didn’t collapse, and life gave us yet another secret experience.
Those little acts of defiance were our great discoveries. They held joy, laughter, friendship, and a sense of courage. We didn’t know then that these tiny rebellions – childhood conspiracies, droplets of disobedience, fleeting moments of joy — would stay with us forever.
Now, looking back through the lens of time, I realize: the great things in life are built from those small “little things” we dared to do when we were told not to. And they taught us the most important lesson — to trust ourselves, to follow our desire, and to feel that freedom begins within.

🇺🇦 Anna Didych

"Breath of Spring"
A diptych — one of my latest works.
I had always dreamed of painting hydrangeas, but choosing a color was nearly impossible. They’re so different — in the shape of their petals, in their hues. So I went with a mix. A blend of colors and varieties of hydrangeas.
This painting isn’t just decoration — it’s a feeling. A state of being one longs to return to.
Hydrangeas carry various meanings across cultures. They are often associated with gratitude, understanding, sincerity, and hope. Sometimes, they’re interpreted as a message: “Remember me.” The flower also symbolizes admiration for women and feminine beauty.
To me, a woman is spring.

🇳🇴 Martine Poppe

Martine Poppe (b. 1988, Norway) lives and works in London and Oslo. Her practice explores the boundaries between abstraction and representation, with climate at its core. Starting from photographs of nature digitally manipulated to the edge of illegibility, she translates these sketches into paintings of quick, regular strokes. The resulting surfaces capture the fleeting qualities of diffracted light, overexposure, and pixelation, creating meditative spaces that feel both personal and universal.
Her series The Earth in Search of Magic Tidings uses photographs of cut flowers from industrial greenhouses worldwide, collaged with images of insects that would never naturally coexist. Referencing baroque vanitas still lifes, climate change, and globalisation, the work reflects on the shared human pleasure of looking at a bouquet. The paintings crystallise this moment—when genetically modified, commercially produced blooms elicit an instinctive aesthetic response—meditating on the contradictory coexistence of our love for nature, our disconnection from it, and its commodification.

🇮🇹 Nadia Tognazzo

I am an Italian watercolorist who lives and works in Milan, Italy. Painting has always been my great passion, since childhood, but it was only in 1989 that I fully dedicated myself to watercolor. Nature has always been one of my main sources of inspiration, and flowers are among my favorite subjects. I love painting them large, enveloping the viewer with their scent and beauty. The colors and shades intertwine as I try to capture the air and light passing through the leaves. What I want to portray is their positive energy, which I hope will involve the observer.
My technique is intuitive and instinctive, and watercolor helps me tremendously with this. I have been a member of the Italian Watercolorists Association since 1994, and in 2015, I joined the prestigious Society of Fine Arts in Milan. I have participated in numerous exhibitions and I teach watercolor courses in Italy and abroad. I am a member of the Jury of the Biennial International Price Fabriano Watercolor (Italy).

🇺🇦 Mariia Duzhuk

I’m an artist and art teacher who is almost never without a paintbrush. My journey in art began in 2014. After six years of study, I earned a Master’s degree and continued creating while discovering another passion — teaching.
In my artistic practice, I’m currently most focused on portraits, architectural sketches, still lifes, and floral compositions. I love capturing fleeting moments and conveying atmosphere — the poetry found in an old façade, in light and shadow, in a sunbeam on the windowsill. Many of my works are born as reflections — a response to inner states or observations of the world around me. Painting, for me, is a way of living, feeling, grounding myself, and finding joy.
“Irises”
This piece was born during a walk after the rain. I saw irises — delicate, with droplets of water on their petals. The air was warm, humid, and fresh; everything around seemed to breathe peace and renewal. I wanted to capture that exact feeling — soft, luminous, both alive and serene. In this work, I focused on light, color transparency, and the fragility of form, to express a state that is hard to put into words, but can be felt through painting.

Artist Bio: Erin Kate Archer
Erin Kate Archer (she/her) is a New York-based artist whose work stands out as a beacon of resilience and solace. Erin's artwork is a soothing amalgamation of the ethereal and the tangible, narrating tales of endurance and the feminine spirit through her mixed-media paintings.
Her portfolio is a testament to her commitment to tranquility amidst struggles with chronic illness and pain. It features immersive fairytale landscapes, celestial pieces, and intricate depictions of femininity, womanhood, and community.
Erin's artistry extends beyond personal expression. Erin's accomplishments include being published in 'She Votes' (2020) and 'Forget Me Not Press - A Cold Winter's Night' (2022), and exhibiting in several prominent NYC art shows in 2023, such as Studio Artego's summer show and The Art of Access benefit show.
Artist Statement: Erin Kate Archer
In my art, I seek to create a haven for those in search of comfort, inspiration, and magic. My work is a celebration of the transformative power of creativity and the essence of femininity, an invitation to explore a world where there is strength in softness, where the challenges of life are met with resilience and grace.
Through my mixed-media paintings, I use color and texture to make a space where viewers can find solace from their struggles. My work is a reflection of my journey, a narrative woven with threads of my own experiences with chronic illness and pain, and a testament to the therapeutic power of art.

About my pastel art
My pastel works combine elements of realism, abstractionism and impressionism, based on keen, direct observation of my subject. I interprate the perceived visual information with varying ratio of conscious calculation and spontaneous experimentation.
My preferred painting genre is still life. It allows me to work undisturbed in silent conversation with the setup and the painting.

🇺🇦 Vera Tsepkova

Vera Tsepkova, educated as an international economist and a certified translator of German and French languages, nowadays - contemporary artist. Her works are inspired by the beauty of everyday life, the subtle nuances of nature, and the unique atmosphere of each moment. 🇫🇷 Taevane

I’m a self-taught collage artist, deeply shaped by an early life immersed in art. My father was a still life oil painter and teacher, giving lessons in a studio at the back of our garden. From a young age, I watched him share his love of light, color, and detail.
At thirteen, I discovered collage as my own medium—and I never stopped. My process is intuitive and emotional, guided by a neurodivergent perspective that sees connections between images, symbols, and stories.
Through collage, I aim to evoke feeling, build bridges between cultures and sensibilities, and offer quiet fragments of light and hope in a complex world. I just made this collage. I know it’s not only flowers, but I thought of Ukraine’s spiritual and Christian heritage. The sparkling flowing water symbolizes renewal, grace, hope, and the continuous flow of life even through hardship.
